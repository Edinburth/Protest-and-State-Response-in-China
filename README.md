# Protest and State Response in China

## 💫 Overview
This is the analytical report for *POLI3148 - Data Science in Politics and Public Administration* at HKU. It investigates the patterns of collective action and the logic of state intervention in China between 2018 and 2025. It begins with an overview of protests in China, then explores how the state selectively applies coercion based on protest characteristics and participant identity, treating state response as an outcome shaped by perceived threats to regime stability.  

## 📊 Data Sources
The analysis utilizes the protest and riots data in China downloaded from the **Armed Conflict Location and Event Data (ACLED)** project. This dataset includes granular information on event dates, locations, participant types, crowd sizes, and textual descriptions of protest motives.  

## 📟 Methodology
Analytical methods include descriptive statistics to map protest distribution, logistic regression to isolate factors influencing intervention, and automated text analysis for motive identification. All data analysis and visualizations were conducted on Python with the help of AI coding assistants.  

## 🔬 Key Findings
- **Protest Characteristics & Geography**: The modal protest in China is peaceful, localized, and heavily concentrated in eastern and southern coastal economic hubs like Shenzhen, Beijing, and Shanghai.  
- **Motives & Seasonality**: Over 82% of all events are driven by labor groups attempting to resolve economic disputes, most notably wage arrears. This specific economic incentive creates a distinct seasonal peak in protest frequency between November and January, ahead of the Chinese New Year.  
- **State Response & Selective Coercion**: The state manages social contention through a logic of "selective coercion" rather than uniform repression, maintaining an overall intervention rate of just 9.7%. When intervention occurs, regular civilian policing is preferred over militarized force.  
- **Predictors of Intervention**: State response is associated with the perceived threat to regime stability. The probability of intervention increases sharply in response to collective violence (riots trigger intervention in over 90% of cases) , larger crowd sizes , highly sensitive geographic locations (e.g., Tibet, Beijing) , and specific actor identities (such as military veterans and ethnic minorities) that pose ideological or physical challenges to the state. 

## 🤔 Limitations
Findings are subject to China’s strict information control and media-based reporting, which may result in data omissions. Additionally, limited by the author's skills, the results are summative and correlational rather than strictly causal.
